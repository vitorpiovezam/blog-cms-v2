For most people, electronic music feels like something very new, almost too modern: festival lights, LED walls, the DJ seeming to be piloting a spaceship. And most of the time, it is just a guy pressing play on the same repetitive tracks from a USB stick while everyone pretends something spiritual is happening.

But when you pull the thread, the whole thing starts way before the club, before techno, before house, before the Brazilian funk "pancadão".

It starts with people messing with electricity and thinking: "what if this thing could also sing?"

## 1919 - Theremin, the instrument you play without touching

The Theremin was invented by Leon Theremin, a Russian physicist, around 1919/1920. And the craziest part is that it feels more like witchcraft than an instrument: two antennas, one controlling the pitch, the other controlling the volume, and you play it by moving your hands in the air.

No keys. No strings. No breath. No touching anything.

And this matters a lot because, before becoming a language for dance floors, electronic music is born as a question: can electricity be expressive? Can it have vibrato, drama, voice, ghost?

The sound feels like an old science fiction soundtrack, because that is exactly what it became later. But before becoming a movie cliche, it was a real discovery: the human body could talk to an electromagnetic field and sound could come out of it.

## 1970 - Ruckzuck and Germany youth in the post-war

Cut to Germany in the late 60s and early 70s. The situation in post-war Germany was awful, and the younger generation was looking for new trends and a new identity. Rock was there, of course, but there was also a group of people trying to escape from copying American blues and create something else. Something more repetitive, colder, more urban, more industrial. People usually call this umbrella krautrock, and inside this movement, Kraftwerk appears.

In 1970, they release "Ruckzuck". It is not yet that robotic, geometric Kraftwerk from "The Man-Machine". There is flute, Hammond organ, and it still feels like a band playing live. But there is already something hypnotic in it. A pulse. An engine. Kraftwerk members do not like these much, and these first three experimental albums are hidden in their discography. Once Ralf Hütter was asked why, and he simply said it was pre-historical work — that they were just "finding Kraftwerk".

The Kraftwerk thing was not only about using synthesizers. It was about thinking of music as a system. Repetition as beauty. Machine as groove. Their coldness, which many people found strange, later became one of the warmest things on the dance floor.

## 1977 - Trans-Europe Express: the train becomes rhythm

"Trans-Europe Express" is one of those moments where the idea is simple and absurd at the same time: turning the experience of a European train into electronic music.

The rail becomes sequence. The locomotive becomes beat. The trip becomes loop.

And here there is a beautiful detail for Brazilian music history: the beat of today's funk carioca has an ancestry that passes through this imagination of rails, engines and machines.

It is not that funk was literally born inside a German train. That would be too simplistic. But the rhythmic cell Kraftwerk put into the world with "Trans-Europe Express" traveled. It landed in hip-hop DJs' hands, became a foundation for "Planet Rock", entered electro, passed through Miami Bass and reached Rio's bailes, where it was remade, accelerated, Brazilianized, sweated out.

In the end, that elegant sound of a European train became one of the many roots of our pancadão. The rail became bass.

## 1978 - Yellow Magic Orchestra: the future also spoke Japanese

Then comes Yellow Magic Orchestra, in Japan, with Haruomi Hosono, Yukihiro Takahashi and Ryuichi Sakamoto. And here things get very interesting because YMO is not just "the Japanese Kraftwerk", even if that comparison always appears.

YMO takes exotica, videogames, advertising, pop, synthesizers, irony, Japanese technology, all together, and builds its own language.

<p align="middle">
  <img alt="Yellow Magic Orchestra in a 1981 press photo" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Yellow%20Magic%20Orchestra%20YMO%20%281981%20Press%20Photo%29.jpg" width="560px">
<br>
  <small>Yellow Magic Orchestra in 1981: pop, technology and synthesizers without asking permission.</small>
</p>

Sakamoto, especially, had this thing of crossing worlds. He could be in electronic pop, classical music, cinema, contemporary art, and somehow it all felt like part of the same conversation.

In 1980 he releases "Riot in Lagos", on the album B-2 Unit, and then the story touches hip-hop in a serious way. I do not think it is fair to say that Sakamoto "created rap". Rap was already being born in the Bronx, in sound systems, with DJs, MCs, and the street itself. But "Riot in Lagos" brought a kind of electronic beat, broken, dry, futuristic, that influenced DJs and producers in electro, hip-hop and techno.

It was the TR-808 appearing as a body. Not as a technical detail, but as a new way of making people move.

## Extras

You cannot tell this story without **Brian Eno** and **Gary Numan**. Eno took the synthesizer out of the lab and into art rock, then production and ambient music, opening the door for machines to sound like mood, not just gimmick. Numan did something else: cold, android pop that actually charted, and suddenly the radio was full of synth. Together they helped drag electronic music from a nerd niche into disco floors and mainstream pop, years before the club took over.

**Kraftwerk** and **YMO** were not a single family tree. The contact points were few, but real: they respected each other, crossed paths in interviews and press, and showed up at each other's concerts when tours lined up, especially when Kraftwerk played Japan and YMO was watching.

The rest of the thread still holds: Theremin in the laboratory, Kraftwerk as cold poetry, YMO as smart pop, the 808 in DJs' hands, Afrika Bambaataa on the street, Miami Bass, and making Rio more poverty classes dance with the Carioca Funk,

## And then today happens

Here is where my view gets pessimistic. A lot of mainstream electronic music today feels less like music culture and more like a consumption machine. I went to XXXExperience rave-party in 2022, thinking that would be an experience to watch the electronic new tendencies. And I just saw drug excess, too many repeated tracks from the same USB-stick logic, very few artists actually doing something great. Sometimes the mainstream festival circuit feels like a market built around people consuming drugs while a predictable soundtrack keeps the machine running.

Funny detail: my friends were waiting for Timmy Trumpet, this guy who supposedly plays trumpet on his tracks. Guess what—the trumpet was pure theater. Not a single damn microphone anywhere near the instrument.


Real electronic music still exists, but you need to dig for it. TSHA, for example, makes electronic/house music with taste, space and feeling. "Demba", with Trio Da Kali, has that rare thing of sounding modern without becoming empty. Future bass, when it moves away from the aggressive dubstep formula, can also be much more melodic and emotional. In Brazil, eceologyk is a very good producer, including trap. And Sylvan Esso are among my favorites because they keep things minimal, physical and alive.

I think I like artists like TSHA and Sylvan Esso for the same reason I like Kraftwerk: they understand minimalism, and they still feel like people actually performing music. Not just a USB stick pretending to be a concert.
